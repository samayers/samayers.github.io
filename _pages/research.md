## Publications

**[Effect of an Intensive Nurse Home Visiting Program on Adverse Birth Outcomes in a Medicaid-Eligible Population: A Randomized Clinical Trial](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2793825)**

with Margaret A McConnell, Slawa Rokicki, Farah Allouch, Nicolas Perreault, Rebecca A Gourevitch, Michelle W Martin, R Annetta Zhou, Chloe Zera, Michele R Hacker, Alyna Chien, Mary Ann Bates, and Katherine Baicker, 2022

*JAMA*

**Abstract:** Improving birth outcomes for low-income mothers is a public health priority. Intensive nurse home visiting has been proposed as an intervention to improve these outcomes. This randomized clinical trial included 5,670 Medicaid-eligible, nulliparous pregnant individuals at less than 28 weeks' gestation, enrolled between April 1, 2016, and March 17, 2020, with follow-up through February 2021. Participants were randomized 2:1 to the Nurse Family Partnership program (n = 3,806) or control (n = 1,864). The program is an established model of nurse home visiting; regular visits begin prenatally and continue through 2 postnatal years. The primary outcome was a composite of adverse birth outcomes (preterm birth, low birth weight, small for gestational age, or perinatal mortality) based on vital records, Medicaid claims, and hospital discharge records. The incidence of the composite adverse birth outcome was 26.9% in the intervention group and 26.1% in the control group (adjusted between-group difference, 0.5% [95% CI, −2.1% to 3.1%]). Assignment to participate in an intensive nurse home visiting program did not significantly reduce the incidence of a composite of adverse birth outcomes. Evaluation of the overall effectiveness of this program is incomplete, pending assessment of early childhood and birth spacing outcomes.

**[Intensive Nurse Home Visiting and Early Childhood Outcomes: A Randomized Clinical Trial](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapediatrics/fullarticle/2834897)**

with Katarina Swanson, Dea Oviedo, R Annetta Zhou, Mary Ann Bates, Katherine Baicker, Alyna T Chien, and Margaret McConnell, 2025

*JAMA Pediatrics*

**Abstract:** The Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP) is an intensive nurse home visiting program designed to improve outcomes for low-income families. This randomized clinical trial enrolled 5,670 Medicaid-eligible pregnant individuals in South Carolina between 2016 and 2020, randomized 2:1 to receive NFP services (n = 3,806) or usual care (n = 1,864), with follow-up through two years postpartum using administrative data. The primary outcome was a composite measure including child mortality and claims related to major injury or abuse/neglect within two years of birth. The incidence of the composite adverse outcome was 27.3% and 26.8% in the intervention and control groups, respectively (adjusted between-group difference, 0.4 percentage points; 95% CI, −2.3 to 3.0). Assignment to intensive nurse home visiting services did not reduce the likelihood of adverse outcomes in early childhood measured through administrative data. More evidence is needed to understand whether delivering intensive home visiting at scale to Medicaid populations influences longer-term developmental outcomes.
